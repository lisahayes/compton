Irrigation Leak Detection & Repair in Compton, CA

Outdoor watering is the biggest discretionary line on a Southern California water bill. A leaking system makes it the biggest involuntary one.

✆ Call (424) 544-0235

In most Southern California homes with a yard, more than half of all water used never enters the house. It goes to landscape, through an irrigation system assembled from plastic pipe, buried valves, and fittings that were never meant to last as long as they are asked to. That budget share is exactly why irrigation leaks punish so efficiently: the system already owns the largest slice of the bill, so a mainline seep or a valve that never fully closes compounds on top of the property's thirstiest line item. And because everything happens underground on a timer, often before sunrise, an irrigation system can hemorrhage for a full season with nobody awake to see it happen.

Mainline versus zone: the first fork in every diagnosis

An irrigation system has two pressure regimes, and the leak's regime decides the search. The mainline, from the point of connection through the backflow device to the valve manifolds, is pressurized always, controller on or off. Loss there shows on the meter around the clock and soaks its neighborhood continuously. Zone lines past the valves hold pressure only while their valve runs, so their failures keep the watering schedule. Our first tests split the system at that fork: meter observation with the controller dark convicts or acquits the mainline, then valve-by-valve isolation runs each zone under watch. The distinction matters commercially too, since a mainline leak wastes water every hour while a zone-side break in the sprinkler laterals wastes it only by appointment.

Valves, the system's soft tissue

Between the regimes sit the valves, and they fail in a way owners rarely suspect: partially. A diaphragm compromised by grit or a solenoid that no longer seats lets a thread of water pass continuously into its zone, too little to pop heads, enough to keep one corner of lawn perpetually damp and the meter perpetually moving. The city's mineral-load water accelerates exactly this failure, scaling diaphragm seats and metering orifices. We rebuild or replace failed valves, clean manifolds, and where a system still runs on decades-old anti-siphon valves, we say plainly when the manifold's era, not one diaphragm, is the problem.

Finding buried failures without plowing the beds

Locating follows the same surface-first discipline as all our below-grade line work: trace the run electronically, listen where pressure allows, use tracer gas where soft soil and plastic pipe mute everything else, and dig once at a mark. Drip systems get their own protocol, since emitter-tubing failures hide under mulch and reveal themselves by flow measurement at the valve more reliably than by any surface evidence. Repairs get bedded and clamped to outlast the original work, and every visit ends with the controller reprogrammed to what the landscape actually needs, because scheduling waste and leak waste read identically on a bill.

From parkway strips to pasture lines

Compton irrigates at two scales. Most of the city runs residential zones and commercial parkway systems along its corridors. Then there is Richland Farms, where agricultural heritage means irrigation infrastructure with real history: long runs sized for orchards and animals, hand-modified across generations, serving lots measured in fractions of an acre. Those systems reward exactly the methodical approach this page describes, because guessing on a 400-foot run is expensive and instruments are not. Whether yours waters a parkway strip or a pasture, the test sequence is the same and the number is (424) 544-0235. Irrigation questions from Compton properties

How do I tell irrigation loss from a house leak?

Find the irrigation shutoff, usually at the point of connection near the meter or backflow device, and close it. If the meter's flow indicator stops, the loss lives in irrigation; if it keeps moving, the house side owns it. Five minutes, no tools.

One zone stays wet for days after it runs. Which part is guilty?

Two usual suspects: a valve passing water slowly into that zone around the clock, or a low-point lateral break draining the zone's volume after each cycle. The wetness schedule and a flow test at the valve separate them quickly.

Is drip irrigation less leak-prone than spray?

Differently prone. Drip loses less water per failure but hides failures better, under mulch, at barbed fittings, in tubing animals chew. Flow measurement at the zone valve is the honest health check for drip.

Do you test backflow preventers too?

We inspect and repair leaks at backflow assemblies, which are common failure points, and coordinate certified testing where the device requires it. A weeping backflow device is both a water loss and a compliance issue worth fixing promptly.
