The leaks a house actually gets

Home leaks sort into a short list, and Compton's housing eras decide which ones you are most likely to meet. Supply failures live in walls and under floors, from pressurized lines beneath the slab in the postwar tracts to tired galvanized in the pre-war blocks. Fixture leaks cluster in kitchens and baths, where a worn faucet body, a failed wax ring, or a corroded angle stop does slow damage inside a cabinet. Drain leaks hide between floors, staining a ceiling only when the shower above runs. And outdoors, hose bibs, irrigation, and aging service lines lose water invisibly into flat coastal-plain soil. One diagnostic visit can test all of it.

What a home visit looks like

We start with your account, because the person who lives with a house knows its sounds and smells better than any instrument. Then the meter test with everything off, splitting the problem into pressurized or not. From there the tools follow the evidence: isolation testing by zone, acoustic and thermal work along suspect runs, dye and fill tests on fixtures and drains, moisture mapping to bound whatever has already gotten wet. You get the findings the same visit, in writing, with the repair priced before anything is opened. If the answer is good news, a condensation issue or a fixture seal, we say so and charge for the diagnosis, not for drama.

Repairs sized to a lived-in house

Residential repair is a discipline of small openings and clean exits. Wall access holes sized to a hand, not a sledgehammer. Slab spot repairs staged so the water is back on by evening. Bathroom work sequenced so one bath stays usable while the leaking one is opened and fixed. Dust barriers where cutting happens, and debris gone when we leave. Bigger interventions, reroutes and repipes, get scheduled around your household rather than dropped on it, with the plan and the price settled first.

Small habits that protect a house between visits

A few minutes a season beats any repair. Learn your main shutoff and prove it turns, because a seized valve during an active leak multiplies every minute of damage. Glance under sinks monthly for the first crust or stain on angle stops. Read your water bill's usage line, not just the total, since consumption history is a leak detector you already pay for. And once a year, watch the meter with the house silent for five minutes. Homeowners who keep those habits call us early, and early calls are the cheap ones.

Homeowner questions we answer every week

Do I need to be home the whole time?

For diagnosis, yes, at least at the start and finish, since we walk you through findings and get approvals. For scheduled repairs, many homeowners hand off after the walkthrough and return for completion. We work either way.

Can you check the whole house while you are here?

Yes, and it is often the smart add. A whole-home leak check covers supply pressure test, fixture inspection, visible drain review, and water heater condition in one pass, useful before remodels, after escrow, or when a bill first creeps.

Will you work with my insurance claim?

We document what adjusters need: cause of loss, photos, moisture readings, and the repair invoice. The coverage decision is between you and your carrier, but our paperwork is built to support a legitimate claim.

My house is a rental I own. Do you coordinate with tenants?

Routinely. We schedule directly with occupants when you authorize it, keep you informed at each step, and send findings and invoices to you. Clear roles, no surprises on either end.
